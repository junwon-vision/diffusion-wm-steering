Naughty Dubins Car

How well does diffusion world model steering work? We study this in a simulated navigation task with stochastic dynamics. Robots can be mischievous—they do not always behave as intended, and may even do the opposite of what we ask. With this in mind, we investigate how effectively we can steer the world model of this naughty Dubins car to imagine possible future trajectories.

Task & Dynamics
The Naughty Dubins Car is a Dubins car with constant speed and an angular velocity control input. There is a circular failure region at the center of the environment. Uniquely, the car exhibits a "naughty" behavior: with a small probability, the sign of its angular velocity action is flipped. This randomness only occurs during turning—when traveling straight, the behavior is always as intended. As a result, executing a turn can cause the car to unexpectedly turn the opposite direction with some probability.

The privileged state is $\mathbf{s}_t = [p_x,\,p_y,\,\theta]$, where the car is controlled by a continuous angular velocity $a_t \in \mathcal{A} = [-a_{\max},\,a_{\max}]$, with $a_{\max} = 1.25\,\mathrm{rad/s}$. The forward speed is fixed at $v = 1\,\mathrm{m/s}$, and the system updates every $\Delta t = 0.05\,\mathrm{s}$. To capture the system's stochasticity, the sign of the control input may be flipped at each step. A red boundary in the right video highlights exactly when the action's sign is flipped, making each occurrence of the "naughty" intervention visually clear.

$$\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = \mathbf{s}_t + \Delta t\,\bigl[\,v\cos\theta_t,\;\; v\sin\theta_t,\;\; \delta_t\,a_t\,\bigr], \qquad \delta_t \in \{-1,\,+1\}$$

Specifically, $\delta_t = -1$ with probability $0.4$ and $\delta_t = +1$ with probability $0.6$. The ground-truth criterion that defines the failure set is given by

$$r(\mathbf{s}) = p_x^2 + p_y^2 - 0.5^2$$

This yields a circular failure region of radius $0.5\,\mathrm{m}$ centered at the origin. We train a diffusion-based world model following the EDM framework using 4000 randomly sampled trajectories, and subsequently learn the criterion function atop the model's latent space. Steering utilizes the learned safety margin as the objective—maximizing it for optimistic, or minimizing it for pessimistic steering. The exponential growth of possible outcomes from the sign-flip stochasticity poses a significant challenge for long-horizon steering.

How well does initial noise optimization work?
We tested steering of naughty dubins car world model with the initial noise optimization, with 5000 randomly sampled evaluation trajectories. The goal is to test if steering can maximize/minimize the criterion (safety margin) while keeping plausibility in imaginations (i.e., do not steer more than the physical limits).
Aggregating over 5000 random evaluation trajectories: the maximum safety margin reached per trajectory (averaged). Dashed line = ground-truth optimistic minimum / pessimistic maximum under true dynamics. Hover any bar for an explanation.

Why is it hard? Unlike single-step Vista / Ctrl-World settings, this is long-horizon. Without effective steering, rollout branches grow exponentially, so best-of-N sampling cannot discover the pessimistic or optimistic modes of the imagined distribution.

Nominal / Best-of-N are effectively indistinguishable, confirming that naive sampling cannot steer this long-horizon imagination.
How well does the regularizer work?
We tested the regularizer on the naughty dubins car world model, with 5000 randomly sampled evaluation trajectories. Spectral density, isotropy, and norm are optimization regularizer terms used during steering. Out-of-distribution score (measured by density estimation via flow matching) is not used for optimization; it is an evaluation-only check for whether the regularizer keeps generations in-distribution.

How robustly can we detect failure events using world model steering?
To evaluate the robustness of failure detection, we tested pessimistic steering on the naughty Dubins car world model, using 5000 randomly sampled evaluation trajectories. Each trajectory is labeled as positive if it has a non-zero probability of entering the failure set under the true dynamics, and negative if—despite the naughty behavior—entry is impossible.

For each case, we steer the model's imagination using just the action sequence and initial state, and classify the trajectory as positive or negative. Ideally, pessimistic steering will imagine failures for positives (TPR↑) without falsely predicting failures for negatives (FPR↓). When steering is performed without the regularizer (green), the optimization tends to overshoot the ground-truth bound: steered noise leaves the in-distribution manifold, predicting margins beyond physical limits—this inflates both TPR and FPR.

Ablations — which regularizer term matters?
Out-of-distribution score of the generated latents. Each ablation removes one term from the regularizer; the rest are kept.
Out-of-distribution score via density estimation — full ablation sweep
Dropping any single regularizer term (no norm, no spectral, no std) lets the density drift above the with reg baseline (≈172). Removing regularization entirely (no reg) sends density to ≈697 — roughly 4× inflation, confirming that each term contributes to keeping steered latents in-distribution.
